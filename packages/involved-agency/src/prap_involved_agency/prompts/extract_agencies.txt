
<agency_extraction_prompt>

<objective>
Extract INVESTIGATING and RESPONDING agencies from police document summaries.
Only extract agencies where the text clearly links an agency name to an action.
Prefer under-extraction over incorrect extraction.
</objective>

<definitions>
INVESTIGATING AGENCY: An agency that investigated the USE OF FORCE or OFFICER MISCONDUCT — NOT the underlying crime.

This is about inter-law-enforcement accountability. We are looking for agencies that reviewed, investigated, or made decisions about WHETHER AN OFFICER'S ACTIONS WERE JUSTIFIED.

- Required pattern: [Agency Name] investigated the shooting / reviewed the use of force / determined the shooting was lawful / declined charges against the officer / conducted autopsy / investigated officer misconduct
- The agency name and the investigation-of-force verb must appear together in the same passage.

VALID investigating actions (reviewing WHETHER FORCE WAS JUSTIFIED):
- Investigated the officer-involved shooting
- Reviewed whether the use of force was within policy
- Determined the shooting was lawful or unlawful
- Declined to file charges against the officer
- Conducted autopsy and determined cause of death
- Investigated officer misconduct or policy violations
- Oversight review of use of force

INVALID investigating actions (investigating the UNDERLYING CRIME — this makes them RESPONDING):
- Investigated the burglary, robbery, assault, domestic disturbance
- Responded to and investigated a call for service
- Interviewed crime witnesses or collected crime scene evidence
- Arrested the suspect

CRITICAL: The agency that investigated the crime the suspect committed is a RESPONDING agency. The agency that investigated WHETHER THE OFFICER'S FORCE WAS JUSTIFIED is the INVESTIGATING agency.

RESPONDING AGENCY: An agency whose officers were involved in or present at the incident.
- Required pattern: Officer [Name] of/from [Agency Name] responded / used force / fired weapon / pursued / was present / secured scene / violated policy
- Also includes agencies whose officers investigated the underlying crime (burglary, robbery, etc.)
- The officer, agency name, and action verb must appear together in the same passage.
</definitions>

<common_agency_types>
These are the typical agency types involved in use-of-force and misconduct cases.
Use this as a checklist — if you see evidence of any of these, look carefully
for both their RESPONDING and INVESTIGATING roles.

TYPICALLY RESPONDING:
- City Police Departments (e.g., Los Angeles Police Department, San Jose Police Department)
- County Sheriff's Departments (e.g., Riverside County Sheriff's Department)
- Highway Patrol (e.g., California Highway Patrol)
- Specialized / Transit / Port Police (e.g., Los Angeles Port Police, East Bay Regional Park District Police)
- State Corrections (e.g., California Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation)

TYPICALLY INVESTIGATING:
- County District Attorney's Offices (e.g., Los Angeles County District Attorney's Office)
- State Departments of Justice (e.g., California Department of Justice)
- Medical Examiners / Coroners (e.g., Los Angeles County Medical Examiner-Coroner, Riverside County Coroner's Office)
- Oversight bodies with investigative authority (e.g., Office of Inspector General, San Francisco Department of Police Accountability)

COMMONLY DUAL-ROLE (both RESPONDING and INVESTIGATING):
- Sheriff's Departments often respond AND investigate via internal affairs or homicide bureau
- Large Police Departments (LAPD, SFPD, Long Beach PD, etc.) often respond AND investigate their own officers via internal divisions (Force Investigation Division, Internal Affairs, etc.)

IMPORTANT: When you extract an agency as RESPONDING, actively re-read the text for evidence
they ALSO investigated the use of force. When you extract an agency as INVESTIGATING, actively
re-read the text for evidence their officers were ALSO present at the incident. If both roles
are supported, list the agency in BOTH responding_agencies AND investigating_agencies.
</common_agency_types>

<core_rule>
CLEAR LINKAGE REQUIRED:
Every extraction must be supported by clear linkage between an AGENCY NAME and an ACTION VERB
within the same passage (paragraph or closely adjacent sentences).

VALID: "Officer Jones of the Glendale Police Department fired his weapon" — agency and action in same statement.
VALID: "Officer Jones, a Glendale Police Department patrol officer, responded to the call. Jones fired his weapon at the suspect." — agency clearly established for this officer in same passage, action follows immediately.
VALID: "The Los Angeles County District Attorney's Office investigated the officer-involved shooting" — agency and action in same statement.
INVALID: Agency mentioned only in a roster, organizational chart, or document header with no connection to incident actions.
INVALID: Agency name appears only in letterhead or footer with no narrative linkage to actions.
INVALID: Officer named in one section of the document, agency named in a completely separate section with no narrative connection.

If you cannot find a passage that clearly connects the agency name to the action, do not extract it.
</core_rule>

<extraction_rules>
RULE 1 — NO FEDERAL AGENCIES:
Skip FBI, DEA, ATF, U.S. Marshals, Border Patrol, DHS, ICE, and all other federal agencies.
NOTE: State-level agencies are NOT federal and SHOULD be extracted. This includes:
- State Departments of Justice (e.g., California Department of Justice)
- State Highway Patrols (e.g., California Highway Patrol)
- State Departments of Corrections (e.g., California Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation)
- State-level investigative agencies (e.g., Arizona Department of Public Safety)

RULE 2 — GEOGRAPHIC IDENTIFIER REQUIRED:
Agency names must start with a city or county name.
- CORRECT: "Los Angeles County District Attorney's Office"
- INCORRECT: "District Attorney's Office" (missing geography)
- If the summary text provides the geographic context elsewhere in the same document, you may add it. If not, reject the agency.
- EXCEPTION: State-level agencies already carry their geographic identifier (e.g., "California Department of Justice").

RULE 3 — PARENT AGENCY ONLY:
Normalize sub-divisions to their parent agency.
- "LASD Internal Affairs Bureau" → "Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department"
- "Orange County Crime Laboratory" → "Orange County Sheriff's Department"
- "LAPD Force Investigation Division" → "Los Angeles Police Department"
- Remove court locations, station names, bureau names from the agency name.

RULE 4 — LAW ENFORCEMENT AND OVERSIGHT AGENCIES ONLY:
Exclude fire departments, EMS, hospitals, and multi-agency task forces.
INCLUDE formal government oversight bodies with investigative authority, such as:
- Office of Inspector General (OIG)
- Department of Police Accountability
- Police Commissions with investigative mandates
These oversight bodies should be classified as INVESTIGATING when they review use of force or misconduct.
</extraction_rules>

<evidence_rules>
Every evidence string MUST follow this pattern:

For RESPONDING: [Officer Name] of [Full Agency Name] [action verb describing incident involvement]
For INVESTIGATING: [Full Agency Name] [action verb describing investigation of use of force or misconduct]

If you cannot write the evidence string in this pattern using information from the text, do not extract the agency.

Maximum 200 characters per evidence string. No quotation marks. Single line. Third person.
</evidence_rules>
