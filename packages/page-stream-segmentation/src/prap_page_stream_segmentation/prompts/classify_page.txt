<background>

    California law (SB-1421 and SB-16) requires law enforcement agencies to disclose records related to certain categories of cases involving peace officers:
    - Sustained findings of misconduct (dishonesty, sexual misconduct, racial bias, excessive force, etc.)
    - Use of force resulting in death or great bodily injury
    - Officer-involved shootings

    These disclosures generate large collections of documents. In order for investigators, attorneys, and oversight bodies to review how incidents were handled, it is important to be able to navigate these documents in a structured way, using document dates, types, purposes, authors/recipients, and other metadata. But when documents are obtained through public records requests or litigation, they are provided without any of the relevant metadata or structure, sometimes in a single large PDF file with many documents concatenated together.

    We seek to recover the relevant structure and metadata by reviewing each page and tracking only the metadata and type/purpose of documents, rather than the substantive content of the documents.

</background>

<investigations and documents>

    The following background information describes the types of documents that may appear in disclosed case files. A single case file may contain documents related to misconduct investigations, use-of-force reviews, officer-involved shooting investigations, or a combination of these.

    MISCONDUCT INVESTIGATIONS:
    An investigation of police misconduct may be conducted by an internal affairs division, a civilian review board, or an independent monitor. The investigation can be initiated by a citizen complaint, an internal complaint, or litigation. The investigating body reviews the allegation and determines whether to open a formal investigation. If they open an investigation, they are required to notify the subject officer(s). The investigation is memorialized in an investigative report, which concludes with findings and possibly disciplinary recommendations. A sustained finding and decision to discipline necessitates additional notifications to the subject officer(s). Similarly, termination of employment requires formal communication to the subject officer(s). Officers can appeal disciplinary actions, generating appeal requests and hearing transcripts. In California, the last level of appeal within a police agency is the "Skelly hearing" or "Skelly conference".

    USE-OF-FORCE CASES:
    When an officer uses force resulting in death or great bodily injury, the incident is documented through police incident reports and supplemental reports by involved and witness officers. The agency typically conducts an internal use-of-force review, which may generate a use-of-force report, response-to-resistance report, or critical incident report. If the subject is injured or killed, the file may include medical records, injury documentation, autopsy or coroner reports, and toxicology reports from the medical examiner's office. Body-worn camera logs and evidence inventories may also be included.

    OFFICER-INVOLVED SHOOTINGS:
    Officer-involved shootings are typically investigated both internally and by the District Attorney's office (or occasionally the Attorney General). The DA's Bureau of Investigation generates investigation reports covering the initial response, walk-through, witness interviews, and officer interviews. The DA issues a review letter or declination memo with findings on whether the shooting was justified. Forensic evidence examination reports (ballistics, firearms testing) may come from the Department of Justice Bureau of Forensic Services. Crime scene investigation reports are prepared by sheriff's or police scientific investigation units. Evidence disposition requests handle the return of firearms and other evidence after the investigation concludes.

    OVERSIGHT AND REVIEW:
    Cases may also be reviewed by the Inspector General, civilian oversight boards, or other external bodies, generating monitoring reports, review board findings, and related correspondence. District Attorney offices may review both shootings and misconduct cases.

    COMMON ACROSS ALL CASE TYPES:
    Investigations often involve interviews with witnesses and involved officers, which generate transcripts. A variety of miscellaneous documents may appear, including correspondence, legal citations or policy references, admonishments, training records, or other personnel documents. Some documents are legally required to be produced during investigations and may be named after the law or precedent that requires their production (examples: "Skelly", "Lybarger", "Miranda"). Police incident reports and supplemental reports documenting the underlying incident are common across all case types.

<task description>
    We have received a large PDF file with possibly many reports and documents concatenated together, related to a law enforcement case (which may involve misconduct, use of force, an officer-involved shooting, or a combination of these). Your job is to classify each page of the document in terms of the type/purpose of the document, and extract any relevant metadata that will help downstream users filter and find specific documents.

    We are interested in METADATA, and NOT substantive content. When extracting dates, we are ONLY interested in the date a document was created, sent, or received, but NOT any dates described in the substantive content of the document. When extracting names, we are ONLY interested in the names of senders, recipients, authors, or subjects of the document, but NOT names mentioned in the substantive content of the document. In the case of interview transcripts, we are interested in the names of the participants in the interview, but NOT names that are mentioned by participants in the interview. When extracting case numbers, we are ONLY interested in identifiers associated with the document itself, such as complaint numbers or case numbers contained in page or document headers, but NOT any case numbers referred to within the substantive content of the document. When extracting agencies or divisions, we are ONLY interested in the agency or division responsible for generating the document (on the letterhead), but NOT agencies or divisions mentioned in the substantive content of the document.

    If the page appears to be a continuation of a document from the previous page, only describe the page as "CONTINUATION", and only provide new metadata if there is new metadata on the page that was not present on previous pages of the same document. Most of the time key metadata only appears on the first page of a document, but occasionally signatures and dates can appear at the end, and these are considered key metadata.

<classification tips>
    - Look for keywords and phrases that, in the context of the given background about how investigations and documents work, indicate the purpose of the document (for example, the word "Skelly" is a hint that this document is related to a Skelly hearing; "Bureau of Investigation" suggests a DA investigation report; "Physical Evidence Examination" indicates a forensic report).
    - Pay special attention to headers, footers, and any formal language that may indicate the document type.
    - Headers and footers often include page numbers, which can help tell if this is the first page of a document or a continuation.
    - Pay attention to contiguity across pages -- a single multi-page document should be labeled with the full classification on the first page, and "CONTINUATION" on subsequent pages, and will not be broken up with different document types in between.
    - Use the "previous page context" provided to help determine if this page is a continuation of the previous page's document. Pages that end mid-sentence are likely to be continued on the next page. Signature blocks or formal closings can indicate the end of a document. The contiguity from the end of the previous page to the start of the current page (accounting for page footers/headers) can help determine if this is a continuation.
    - Just because a page type matches the page type of the previous page does NOT mean it is a continuation. Use context clues to determine if a page is a continuation from the previous page, or the start of a new document of the same type. For example, if the current page and the previous page both appear to be transcripts, but the current page has a different interview date or interview participant names, then it is likely a new document rather than a continuation. Page headings often provide helpful context clues. A page beginning mid-sentence is likely a continuation.
    - Documents can come from many different agencies and entities: police departments, sheriff's offices, district attorney offices, the Department of Justice, medical examiner/coroner offices, the Inspector General, civilian oversight boards, and others. The letterhead or header typically identifies the source agency.
